How can a shape be infinitely detailed?

Ferns, coastlines, and the Mandelbrot set — all grown from a rule you could write on a napkin.

Pick up a head of romanesco — that lime-green alien cousin of broccoli — at the grocery store. Snap off one of the little cones. In your hand is a smaller romanesco, bristling with even smaller cones, each of those a tinier romanesco still. You could keep going until your fingers gave out before the pattern did.

Nature does this everywhere. A fern frond is built from little fronds. A tree is a trunk that splits into branches that split into twigs — each twig a miniature tree. Your own lungs branch into bronchi into bronchioles, the same forking rule fifteen times over. Lightning, river deltas, blood vessels, snowflakes, the jagged edge of a coastline: all of them somehow look the same at every zoom level.

Here's the puzzle. Smooth shapes — circles, cubes, the stuff of school geometry — get simpler as you zoom in; a big enough circle looks flat. These natural shapes do the opposite: zoom in and you find more structure, not less. How does nature pack endless detail into a finite thing? And — the part we'll really chase — can we make that detail ourselves, from a rule simple enough to fit on a napkin?

1 · What even is a fractal?

Start with the one idea everything else hangs on: self-similarity. A fractal is a shape whose parts look like the whole — not roughly, not once, but again and again as you keep magnifying.

Think of a Russian nesting doll, except instead of one smaller doll inside, each doll contains two copies of itself, each tilted a bit. Open one and you get two slightly smaller trees. Open those and you get four. The "tree" isn't really the trunk — it's the rule: a branch is just a smaller tree, rotated.

Drag the sliders below. The angle changes how the copies tilt; the depth is how many times we apply the rule before stopping. Watch how a couple of numbers conjure something that looks unmistakably alive.

That's the whole trick of a fractal, in miniature: the complexity isn't in the rule, it's in the repetition. The instructions stay tiny. You just follow them all the way down.

Well, ackshually 🧐

Our tree is exactly self-similar — each part is a perfect scaled copy. Real trees (and ferns, and coastlines) are only statistically self-similar: a zoomed-in piece has the same character as the whole, not the identical shape. That looser kind is what makes nature look natural instead of mechanical — and it's still very much a fractal.

2 · Infinite detail from one tiny rule

Let's make the "follow it all the way down" part concrete with the most famous starter fractal, the Koch curve. The rule is almost insultingly simple:

Take a straight line. Replace its middle third with a triangular bump. Now do the same to every straight piece you just made. Repeat forever.

One step turns 1 segment into 4. The next turns those 4 into 16, then 64, then 256… Slide the depth up and watch a boring straight line sprout an endlessly crinkled edge — using nothing but that single replacement.

Look at what just happened. After n steps the curve has 4n segments, each (⅓)n as long as the original. Multiply those together and the total length is (4/3)n — it grows by a third every single step, with no ceiling. The Koch snowflake fences off a perfectly finite patch of area behind a border of infinite length. That should feel a little wrong. Hold onto that feeling — it's the door into the strangest idea here.

3 · Wait — how long is the coastline?

In 1967 the mathematician Benoît Mandelbrot asked a question that sounds like a child's and turns out to have no good answer: how long is the coastline of Britain?

You'd think you just look it up. But measuring depends on the size of your ruler. With a 100 km ruler you stride past every bay and inlet and get one number. With a 1 km ruler you trace into each bay — and find a longer coast. With a 1 m ruler you wrap around every boulder; longer still. The closer you look, the more wrinkles you resolve, and the total only ever goes up.

Our Koch coastline lets you watch it happen. The slider is your ruler length. Each straight segment is one ruler laid down. As you shrink the ruler, count climbs, and the measured length climbs right along with it — with no value it's settling toward.

This is the coastline paradox, and it's not a measuring error you can fix with a better tape measure. The length genuinely diverges. Which raises an uncomfortable question: if "length" is the wrong ruler-independent way to describe how big this thing is… what's the right one?

This wasn't a thought experiment. In the 1950s the meteorologist Lewis Fry Richardson, digging into what makes neighbouring countries go to war, noticed that reference books couldn't even agree on border lengths: Spain listed its border with Portugal as 987 km, while Portugal called the very same border 1,214 km. Neither was lying — they'd just walked it with different-sized dividers. Mandelbrot seized on Richardson's data and gave the effect its real name: these coasts and borders have a fractional dimension greater than 1, and that number — not any single "length" — is what's genuinely stable about them.

4 · So what dimension is it?

Here's the leap. The right description isn't a length — it's a dimension. And a fractal's dimension doesn't have to be a whole number.

First, a way of thinking about ordinary dimension that has nothing to do with "number of directions." Ask instead: when I shrink a shape, how many copies do I need to rebuild the original?

  • A line, shrunk to ⅓ its length, takes 3 copies to rebuild. 3 = 31.
  • A square, shrunk to ⅓ on each side, takes 9 copies. 9 = 32.
  • A cube, shrunk to ⅓, takes 27. 27 = 33.

The exponent — 1, 2, 3 — is the dimension. So dimension is just the number D that makes "copies = scaleD" true. Don't panic; solving for it is one line:

D = log Nlog s

where N is how many little copies you get, and s is the factor you shrank by. (Any base of log works — it cancels.)

Now point that at the Koch curve. Zoom in by 3× (s = 3) and you find N = 4 copies of the whole curve. So its dimension is log 4 / log 3 ≈ 1.26. Not 1, not 2 — somewhere in between. That fractional number is exactly what the coastline paradox was groping at: the curve is too crinkly to be a 1-dimensional line, but too thin to fill 2-dimensional area. Its roughness is the 0.26.

"But a real coastline isn't made of exact copies," you might object — and you're right. For messy shapes there's a more general trick called box-counting: drop a grid of boxes of size ε over the shape, count how many boxes it touches, then shrink ε and count again. For a smooth line the count grows like 1/ε; for a filled patch, like 1/ε². For a coastline it grows somewhere in between — and that exponent is its dimension. Britain's west coast clocks in around 1.25; the fjord-shredded coast of Norway, closer to 1.5. Roughness, made into a number.

Pick a shape below and watch the formula land on a number between the integers.

Well, ackshually 🧐

What we're computing is the similarity dimension, which is easy because these shapes are made of exact copies. For messier sets (real coastlines, the boundary of the Mandelbrot set) mathematicians use the box-counting and Hausdorff dimensions. They agree with this one on nice self-similar fractals and generalize it to everything else. "Fractal," loosely, means a shape whose dimension is larger than its plain old topological dimension.

5 · The Mandelbrot set: a universe from z² + c

So far every fractal was built by explicitly copying a shape. The most famous fractal of all is built a completely different way — and the rule is even smaller. It works on points in the plane, where each point c is treated as a number you can square and add. Pick a point, start at zero, and repeat one step:

zn+1 = zn2 + c

For some points c, the numbers stay small forever, politely orbiting near zero. For others they blow up to infinity almost immediately. Color a point black if it stays bounded, and shade it by how fast it escapes if it doesn't. That's the entire recipe. The black region is the Mandelbrot set.

That dead-simple feedback loop produces what's been called the most complicated object in mathematics: spirals, lightning, seahorse tails, and — crucially — tiny perfect copies of the whole set buried infinitely deep along its edge. Click anywhere to dive in. (No fast animation; each click just renders the new view.)

Notice it's not exactly self-similar like the tree or the Koch curve — the little copies are warped, wreathed in fresh filigree, never quite the same twice. It's self-similar in spirit, not in photocopy. One line of arithmetic, iterated, holds an endless and endlessly varied world.

Astonishingly, almost nobody had seen this before 1980, when Benoît Mandelbrot — with access to IBM's computers — first plotted it and found that the scattered "islands" dusted around the main body were really joined to it by filaments too thin for the printer to draw. Every point c also has its own twin fractal, a Julia set, and the Mandelbrot set turns out to be a perfect index of them: a master map of an infinite family of fractals, all conjured by that same one-line rule.

Well, ackshually 🧐

How crinkled is its edge? In 1991 Mitsuhiro Shishikura proved the boundary has fractal dimension exactly 2 — the most a curve in the plane can have. The border of the Mandelbrot set is so infinitely wrinkled that, in a precise sense, it nearly fills area despite being a one-dimensional outline. About as rough as roughness gets.

6 · Order from pure randomness

One last way to make a fractal — and it's the strangest, because it runs on chance. It's called the chaos game, and the rules take ten seconds to learn:

Mark three dots — the corners of a triangle. Drop your pencil anywhere. Now repeat: pick a corner at random, slide your pencil halfway toward it, and make a dot. Again. Again.

Every dot lands at a spot chosen by pure coin-flips, so the result should be a formless smear. It isn't. Press play and watch order precipitate out of the randomness — the Sierpiński triangle, assembled from nothing but chance.

That's the whole thesis of this piece, sharpened to its point: you don't need a designer placing the detail, and you don't even need determinism. A simple rule, repeated — even a random one — is enough to summon structure that's intricate at every scale.

7 · Why should I care?

Because fractals are how nature solves a recurring problem: pack a huge amount of something into a small space, cheaply. When you need maximum surface, reach, or coverage on a budget, branching-and-repeating beats any smooth shape — so evolution and engineering both keep rediscovering it.

  • Lungs & blood vessels
    Branching fits ~70 m² of gas-exchange surface inside your chest.
  • Rivers & deltas
    Drainage networks branch to collect water from every acre of land.
  • Fractal antennas
    Crinkling a wire packs many wavelengths in — the antenna hiding in your phone.
  • CGI landscapes
    Films and games grow mountains, clouds, and coastlines with fractal noise.

The same math turns up in image compression, in models of how prices lurch around in markets (Mandelbrot again, warning that wild swings are normal, not freak events), and in the antenna quietly receiving this very page. Once you have the eye for it, the branching is everywhere.

8 · Now you predict it

Your turn 🔮

You just watched the chaos game conjure the Sierpiński triangle — three copies of itself, each half the size, one in each corner with the middle left empty, forever. Here's the real test of whether the model is yours: without peeking back at the dimension demo, work out its dimension from scratch.

You now have everything you need. Using D = log N / log s, with N = 3 copies and s = 2 (each is half-size, so you'd need to magnify 2× to rebuild the original) — what's its dimension? Bigger or smaller than the Koch curve's 1.26? Work it out, then reveal.

D = log 3 / log 2 ≈ 1.585. Higher than the Koch curve — which fits: the Sierpiński triangle spreads through 2D space more thoroughly than a single crinkly line does, so it sits closer to dimension 2. If you predicted "between 1 and 2, and bigger than 1.26," you've got the model. 🎉 And notice — this is the very triangle the chaos game built from random dots, now drawn by exact recursion instead. Same fractal, two completely different recipes.

The bigger picture

For two thousand years geometry meant Euclid: lines, circles, smooth idealized forms. But as Mandelbrot put it, "clouds are not spheres, mountains are not cones, coastlines are not circles, and bark is not smooth." The real world is rough, and roughness has its own geometry — one where dimensions are fractions and the interesting action is the same at every scale. Mandelbrot coined the very word fractal for it in 1975, from the Latin fractus — "broken."

The throughline of everything here is almost suspiciously small: a simple rule, applied to its own output, over and over. A branch is a smaller tree. A segment becomes four. A point gets squared and shifted. From rules that fit on a napkin you get coastlines of infinite length, dimensions between the integers, and a black beetle of a set with a whole cosmos along its edge.

So go back to that piece of romanesco in your hand. It isn't doing anything mystical. It's following a rule — grow a smaller copy of yourself, at an angle, and let that copy do the same — and simply not stopping. Now you can see the napkin behind it. And once you can see it there, you'll start seeing it in the fern on the windowsill, the river on the map, the lightning in the storm, and the branching tree of blood vessels behind your own eyes.
